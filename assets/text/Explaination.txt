Vishnu’s elevated status within the social and religious hierarchy is not simply a reflection of inherent supremacy, but the outcome of sustained theological organization and institutional support. Brahmin scholars, through the development of Purāṇic literature and Vaishnavite traditions, shaped a cosmology in which Vishnu became the preserver and central stabilizing force. His narratives were expanded, systematized, and circulated widely, allowing his worship to become deeply embedded in temple practices and ritual life. This rise was closely tied to processes of Sanskritization, where diverse regional beliefs were absorbed into a broader, standardized framework. Local deities and traditions were often reinterpreted as forms or avatars of Vishnu, enabling a unifying but hierarchical structure. His prominence, therefore, reflects a strategic consolidation of belief systems that aligned spiritual authority with emerging social and religious institutions.

Siva

Siva’s position within the religious hierarchy emerges from a different, though parallel, process of integration and reinterpretation. Originally associated with more ambiguous, ascetic, and non-conformist traditions, Siva was gradually incorporated into Brahminical theology through the expansion of Shaivite texts and temple networks. Agamic practices and philosophical systems helped formalize his worship, transforming a figure rooted in marginal and liminal spaces into one of central cosmic significance. Rather than displacing his earlier associations, this process reframed them—absorbing folk, tribal, and regional practices into a broader Sanskritic structure. Through this theological negotiation, Śiva came to embody both transcendence and authority within an ordered cosmology. His elevated status thus reflects a deliberate accommodation and restructuring of diverse traditions into a cohesive, yet stratified, religious system.

In the Mahabharata, Parashurama is portrayed as a divine force who repeatedly destroys arrogant Kshatriya rulers, even wiping out their class multiple times to punish abuse of power. This role as an ultimate enforcer of cosmic justice and restorer of order elevates him above ordinary gods and mortal kings in the social and moral hierarchy.

Saraswati

In the Rigveda, Saraswati is positioned at the center of Brahminical ritual culture as the goddess of speech (Vāc), sacred knowledge, and priestly inspiration, directly linking her to the authority of Vedic wisdom.
As noted in Vedic scholarship such as Jan Gonda’s work on Vedic literature, her presence within the priestly corpus elevates her as a divine embodiment of learned authority rather than a peripheral folk deity.
Because she is embedded in the Brahminical framework of sacred language and ritual knowledge, her status among gods is established through association with intellectual and spiritual hierarchy, placing her above localized or non-textual deities in the religious order.

Durga

Durga occupies a highly elevated and mass-centered social position in Hindu worship, as evidenced by the enormous congregational devotion during festivals such as Navratri, where tens of millions of devotees visit temples in a single state, reflecting her role as a dominant public deity of collective worship.
This scale of participation positions her as one of the most socially expansive deities in Hinduism, whose authority is not confined to household ritual or localized practice but expressed through large-scale, state-wide devotional movements that cut across caste and regional boundaries. As a form of Shakti, her worship consolidates both institutional temple networks and popular religiosity, reinforcing her status as a supreme protective force whose presence is activated through collective, public, and highly visible acts of devotion.

Shrinathji is associated with a strong base of patronage from merchant communities such as Oswal Jains, Agarwal Vaishyas, and Gujarati Banias, as evidenced in temple records and donation inscriptions from Nathdwara. This positions Shrinathji’s social stature as closely tied to mercantile wealth and influence, where devotional authority is reinforced through strong lay patronage rather than exclusive caste ownership.

Pabuji 

The worship of Pabuji, as described in studies of Pabuji Ki Phad, operates outside Brahmanical ritual authority, where his divine status is mediated through Nayak Bhopa performers rather than Brahmin priests, placing him within a parallel but socially stratified religious system.
The Phad tradition shows that his divinity is publicly enacted through caste-marked performance communities, where lower-caste Bhopa narrators serve as ritual specialists, reflecting a decentralised but hierarchically structured form of sacred authority.
Because his worship is rooted in pastoral and agrarian communities rather than elite Sanskritic institutions, his religious authority reflects a socially grounded form of divinity shaped by caste position and occupational identity.
At the same time, his mythic role as a heroic protector of cattle and community order elevates him within local cosmology, but this elevation remains embedded in regional caste-based performance traditions rather than pan-Indian Brahmanical hierarchy.

Hanuman:

Interpretations of Hanuman’s social stature draw on readings of the Ramayana that identify the Vanaras as indigenous forest-dwelling communities, suggesting that his origins lie outside elite Vedic society and within marginalized or tribal groups. His constant posture of devotion to Rama—often depicted kneeling or at his feet—has been read as reflecting hierarchical, patron–client relationships analogous to caste structures, positioning him within a subordinate yet loyal role. At the same time, contemporary claims linking Hanuman to Dalit and Adivasi identities, including shared clan names and movements to reclaim temple worship, recast him as a figure of subaltern belonging, showing that his status among gods is actively negotiated through social and political contexts.

Mariamman

Mariamman is not merely a localized “folk” deity but a figure whose worship actively challenges the idea of Hinduism as a single, unified, Brahmanical tradition. As Javier Gonzalez Diez argues, her cult represents a dynamic religious expression of middle and lower castes, foregrounding practices and beliefs that are often marginalized in dominant narratives. Its continued strength and expansion in urban South India further demonstrate how such traditions adapt to changing social contexts, revealing the deep connections between religious practice and caste-based social structures, and highlighting Hinduism’s internal diversity rather than a fixed hierarchy.
